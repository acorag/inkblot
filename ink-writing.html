And then another dot, dot...

For the rest of us literary mortals that don't sell books by the pound (about half a kg for all you metric fans), writing can be a lot of fun - and the fun starts with an outline. You can make life easy and build your outline by asking and answering a series of questions. It is like a Spanish Inquisition game... minus all the fear and torture. Plus, you can actually expect this game; and you get to live through it even if you change your answers later. What a fun game!

Outline help: Start Small

I know. Even starting a simple outline can feel simply daunting... but using a simple chart (like the simple chart below) can simplify the creation of your simple outline, because it asks simple questions about your simp- er... unwritten story.

Simple!

It is okay to start with a one word answer. You can expound on that word later. You can also use multiple answers. You have complete creative control.

6 Questions

That is to say, "who?" "what?" "when?" "where?" "why?" and after all of those questions have been answered, you can go back and fill in the periodic "how?s." You have probably realized by now that my favorite writing tools are questions. For example, why is the chart header silver and orange on this pink website? That is a good question. The answer? Because.

Who?

Develop your characters! Write your story about a person or humanize a place, thing, or idea. Examples would be a point of view of a town, a shoe, or an emotion. Writing from the point of view of an object was popularized back in the 1800s and is actually Inky's favorite writing style.

What?

I once read somewhere that George Lucas wrote Star Wars because he wanted to write a space opera. So the first question he asked was "where."

At some point you will have to define what happens in your story. Sometimes you can answer this question last once you know the when, where, and who. But the bulk of your story is going to be answered here, so this question weighs a lot more than any other question, but it is admittedly one of the most ambiguous.

The "what" answer is going to be an event. In sequence, or individualized, events will move your story forward - or back if it is about time travel. Events can be unique to each character or they can be shared. I have noticed a trend of enjoyable stories where an event or a series of events link unconnected characters that may not necessarily ever meet. This concept adds depth to your story.

A useful tactic is to create a chart that connects characters to an event. So at the time of the event, record in the chart each character's name, location, and what they were doing at the moment of the event. Are you getting some writing ideas already? Me too! Isn't this exciting!?

When?

Probably one of the easiest questions, because the choices are limited to past, present, future. You can pick one, or use a combination of all three. This is a crucial question for anyone writing an epoch.

Where?

This question isn't as open ended as "what" but it is probably the closest. If you can think of a place, you can write about it. A story can take place on a subatomic particle, in the nucleus of a cell, or in an alternate reality. It is all up to you!

Why?

Answering this question will give your story depth and dimension. Even if you don't directly answer why something happened in your story, you can still allude to the answer or imply it. Not directly answering "why" is often what keeps people reading. Letting the reader decide the answer can sometimes be a rewarding experience.

As long as you know the answer as to "why" before you begin writing, you can write in a way that lets the answer bleed through in your writing, like ink through paper - slow and frustrating. This writing method is very enjoyable to read, in my opinion.

Considering that nature and physics are constant and dependable, answering "why" is easy when writing about reality. Venturing into fantasy removes the inhibitions of reality, but fantasy still needs its own rules for a story to remain interesting - or at least comprehensible. So keep that in mind.

This is the point where questions are required to be combined in order to write a decent story. You will need to answer a "why" question for every "what" answer (event) you write into your story. This leads to back stories, and prequels, and dramatic, emotional scenes. Eventually leading into our next segway...

How?

Answering this question is going to provide all the dialogue and action in your story. The "how" answers are where you write in all the details. This is where the veritable rubber meets the road.
